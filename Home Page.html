OLIVE RIDLEY TURTLES NEST ON LANKA BEACHES
ONLY LISTED AS VULNERABLE

Between September and November olive ridley turtles lay their eggs on a variety of beaches in Sri Lanka including Kosgoda Yala and other sporadic locations
Due to the low estimated population of 800,000 olive ridley turtles this species is only designated as vulnerable

Why are the Sea Turtles important?



Coral reefs are home to Hawksbills, which eat a handful of species of sea sponges to increase the variety of life on the reef. Without Hawksbills, sponges can overgrow and suffocate slow-growing corals, causing them to die. Beach turtles also have a positive influence on water by depositing their eggs in the sand, providing important nutrients that nourish dune vegetation and help to prevent coastal erosion

What can we do to save the Sea Turtles of
Sri Lanka?




  1. Reduce marine debris that could ensnare or unintentionally ingest
    sea turtles
  2. To keep our beaches and ocean clean, take part in coastal cleanups
    and cut back on your use of plastic
  3. Bring shopping bags and reusable water bottles with you.
    Avoid releasing balloons into the air because they will
    probably end up in the ocean where sea turtles may
    mistake them for prey and eat them
  4. Keep sea turtle nesting beaches dark and secure
  5. Turn off, cover, or reroute any lights that can be seen from
    the beach. Lights confuse young sea turtles and deter females
    from nesting on the beach from coming there to lay their eggs
